01 — Chanel — Beverly Hills, CA Overview

49,200 SF across three floors, priced from 2007 base plans.

Preliminary budgetary estimate for the Beverly Hills office and workshop renovation. Scope confirmed as finishes-only with six-phase half-floor rollout. Formal drawings not yet issued.

This document is a rough costing exercise for Chanel. It is not a formal bid. The number carried here is a defensible ballpark built to close the gap between the $1M Ops figure and the $5M President figure circulating inside client leadership. Jacquelyn Albano requested a credible midpoint before drawings are commissioned. The grand total lands at $4,186,770.

The property covers 49,200 SF across three floors in Beverly Hills. Base plans date to 2007. The master watchmaker has blue-lined proposed wall moves directly on those plans. Three competing schematics remain in play for Floor 2. Per follow-up with RVP Chris, the active scope has been refined to Floors 1 and 2, with Floor 3 carried in the rollup for leadership reference only. Floor 1 is largely storage. Floor 2 is the primary watchmaker renovation.

The estimate assumes finishes-only work with no permit. Interior demolition, wall reconfiguration, electrical relocation, new flooring, paint, and ACT patch across the renovated footprint. Specialty stainless workstations and jeweler benches fall to a separate vendor. Architectural drawings are client-owned. Lux Brand Co supports with build-spec inputs only.

Six phases run in half-floor increments with a one-week overlap between adjacent phases. Demolition is performed off-hours to protect continuous watchmaker, jeweler, polishing, and packaging activity. Framing, finishes, and electrical run on standard hours. Overhead is carried at 12%. Contingency is carried at 5%. Wall linear footage — the single largest variable — is held at 250 LF per floor pending schematic RCP.

03 — Method Approach

Six phases, half-floor increments, one-week overlap, off-hours demo.

Demolition performed off-hours to protect continuous watchmaker, jeweler, and ops activity. Framing and finishes proceed on standard hours to hold labor cost.

The approach holds two variables stable simultaneously: the pricing floor and the operational floor. Demolition absorbs the disruption risk by running off-hours. Construction absorbs the cost risk by running on standard hours. The phasing cadence carves the floorplate into halves so watchmaker, jeweler, polishing, and packaging teams always have a working half to occupy. The method was built to answer a client leadership question — whether this work can happen without shutting the building — with a number rather than a narrative.

01

Six-Phase Half-Floor Rollout

Each floor splits into two halves. Six phases sequence through Floors 1, 2, and 3 with approximately one week of overlap between adjacent phases to keep continuous watchmaker, jeweler, and ops activity unbroken.

02

Off-Hours Demo, Standard-Hours Build

Demolition runs outside business hours to minimize disruption and contain confidentiality risk. Framing, finishes, and electrical run on standard hours. Labor cost is held at standard rates where the scope allows.

03

Off-Hours Walkthrough and Matterport

Initial walkthrough and Matterport scan are performed off-hours per client request. The project remains invisible to broader staff until leadership approves the work and signs the scope.

04

Open-Shop Trade Base

Springfield is confirmed open shop. LBCO leverages its vetted North Central New Jersey trade base along the Short Hills corridor for pricing competitiveness and familiar-hands quality on finish work.

05

Build-Spec Support to Architect

LBCO contributes a detailed build spec derived from stakeholder input. The architect focuses on layout refinement rather than finish-level question-and-answer, which compresses the design timeline.

06

Stakeholder Input Hub

A shared micro-site collects asynchronous input from Chris, Brian, and other stakeholders. Revisions consolidate in one place. The telephone-game risk across departments drops substantially.

07

Furniture Scope Carved Out

GC scope remains color-box only. A separate furniture vendor proposes workstations, jeweler benches, and placement. Base pricing stays clean. Change-order exposure on specialty equipment stays with the equipment owner.

09 — Constraints Guardrails

Two constraints hold the $4.19M number honest.

Numbers are budgetary until an architect issues a schematic RCP. Furniture and specialty equipment sit outside this document entirely.

01 — Drawings

No formal drawings yet, numbers are budgetary.

The estimate is built from 2007 base plans plus hand-marked sketches from the master watchmaker. Wall linear footage is assumed at 250 LF per floor. That figure is the largest variable in the model and will shift materially once an architect issues even a schematic RCP and demo plan. Any movement in wall count translates into predictable movement in labor, framing, electrical routing, and ACT patch. The number carried here is defensible. It is not final.

02 — Scope Boundary

Furniture and equipment fully excluded.

Stainless steel workstations, jeweler benches, and all specialty equipment are owned by a separate vendor. The $4,186,770 GC base is color-box only. The client should budget furniture and specialty workstations in parallel to this document, not inside it. That separation keeps GC change-order exposure low and preserves the option to run vendor selection for benches on its own timeline without disturbing the construction schedule.
