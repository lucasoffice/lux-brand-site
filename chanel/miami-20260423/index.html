01
Overview

Chanel boutique buildout, Miami.

Static construction scope covering architectural finishes, millwork, and MEP coordination. Rough costing exercise issued ahead of formal bid.

Chanel. Miami. Ground-floor boutique buildout, 4,200 gross square feet, program year 2026. The project was scoped against an occupied retail envelope and sequenced around Chanel Miami trading hours and the landlord’s tenant coordination manual. LBCO priced the work to the static drawing set issued at the date of this exercise, with the understanding that the architectural finish schedule, millwork shop drawings, and MEP point assignments remain subject to Chanel design review. Figures were built from first principles, not from adjusted historicals, and each line carries a documented assumption.

Scope was organized into three phases: demolition and rough-in, finishes and millwork, punch and turnover. Architectural finishes were priced to Chanel global retail standard — beige and black natural stone, leather-wrapped wall panels, bouclé upholstery at the fitting suites, and cast-in-place terrazzo at the entry vestibule. Millwork was priced as a coordinated package with a single Chanel-qualified fabricator, with installation supervised by the LBCO site superintendent. MEP coordination was resolved to the extent the current set allows; long-lead equipment and specialty lighting procurement windows were flagged and carried as allowances pending formal confirmation. Mock-ups for stone set, leather reveals, and terrazzo pour were carried into the general conditions line.

This document is a rough costing exercise. It is not a formal bid, not a guaranteed maximum price, and not a construction contract. Figures represent order-of-magnitude pricing against the current drawing set and will be revised against the issued-for-construction package. The next milestone is a joint site walk with the Chanel construction lead, the LBCO superintendent, and the landlord representative, followed by pricing reconciliation against the issued permit set. All information contained herein is confidential and prepared solely for Chanel retail planning review. Distribution outside that circle was not authorized.

Natural stone, Chanel-qualified millwork, and specialty lighting were identified as the three long-lead positions on the critical path. Quoted lead times ran fourteen to eighteen weeks from approved shop drawings.

A procurement release sequence was drafted to fire ahead of permit issuance, preserving the October turnover assumption against the current schedule posture.

iv — Observation

Documentation Posture

Deliverables were structured for Chanel retail planning review — forwardable, archivable, and aligned to the global standard submittal template. The daily log, RFI register, and submittal schedule open at mobilization and remain live through closeout.

Cost reporting cycles weekly against the approved line-item budget, with variance flags triggered at two percent deviation.

03
Approach

Three phases, after-hours coordination, zero retail disruption.

Phasing preserves adjacent boutique operations. Work sequenced around Chanel Miami trading hours and the landlord tenant coordination manual.

3.1 — Phasing

Three phases, sequenced without overlap at the trade face.

Three sequential phases — demolition and rough-in, finishes and millwork, punch and turnover. Each phase closed against a documented checklist before the next opened. Phase boundaries overlapped by two to three working days to preserve critical path, with the overlap limited to non-conflicting trades.

Trade stacking held to four concurrent trades in the space. Millwork install sequenced after stone set, lighting rough, and final paint. Punch and final cleaning held in isolation from any active trade work. No concurrent wet and dry trades permitted in the fitting suite zone.

A weekly look-ahead drove the trade call. Three-week rolling schedule issued every Monday to Chanel retail planning and the landlord tenant coordinator.

3.2 — Hours

After-hours for loud trades, standard hours for finishes.

Demolition, core drilling, and loud MEP rough-in scheduled as after-hours work between 10pm and 6am. Finish trades, stone set, and millwork installation scheduled during standard mall construction hours, seven to five, Monday through Friday. Saturday supervision included for the duration of the millwork install window to preserve fit-and-finish quality.

All work aligned to the landlord tenant coordination manual and the Chanel boutique trading calendar. Noise, dust, and vibration monitoring logged daily, with readings submitted weekly to the landlord.

Holiday blackout windows observed against Chanel Miami trading peaks — no active trade work during the last two weeks of November or the final week of December.

3.3 — Logistics

Off-site staging, scheduled deliveries, protected storefront.

Deliveries staged through the landlord-designated service corridor and loading dock three. Stone and millwork routed under scheduled time windows with two-hour arrival slots held against the landlord dock calendar. Temporary barricade deployed at the storefront on day one with Chanel-approved graphics installed by the brand environment team.

Material staging held off-site at the LBCO Miami warehouse and released to the job site only as trade partners called for it. Zero on-site storage of long-lead stone or millwork prior to installation window.

Fixture and finish protocol deferred to Chanel global retail standards for all visible assemblies. Every exposed edge, reveal, and miter signed off by the LBCO superintendent against the published Chanel quality benchmark before trade release.

08
Guardrails

What LBCO will not do without written Chanel approval.

Material substitutions, subcontractor swaps, schedule compression — all held to written client sign-off. Framed as explicit constraints, not capabilities marketing.

8.1 — Material & Subcontractor Integrity

No substitutions without written approval.

LBCO will not substitute any specified stone, leather, terrazzo mix, paint, or hardware without written Chanel design sign-off, even in the case of confirmed long-lead delay. The procurement posture is built to absorb schedule risk at the pre-construction stage, not at the finish stage.

LBCO will not swap a qualified subcontractor mid-project without written Chanel approval. The trade roster proposed at award is the trade roster delivered at turnover.

Prior luxury retail deliveries at Aventura, Bal Harbour, and the Miami Design District were executed under the same protocol. No exception was requested or granted on any prior engagement.

8.2 — Miami Execution Capacity

No schedule compression at the quality line.

LBCO will not compress the finishes and millwork phase to recover prior-phase delays without written Chanel approval. Mock-up sign-off, cure times, and punch walk cadence are held as fixed quality gates. Compression recovery is pursued through procurement acceleration, not through trade stacking.

Miami execution capacity was sized to absorb the current schedule without recourse to trade stacking — a full-time superintendent, a dedicated project manager, and four qualified trade partners already under active agreement in the local market.

Recent local deliveries include three comparable luxury retail projects completed within the last eighteen months. Trade partner network, landlord relationships, and after-hours access protocols are current and documented.
