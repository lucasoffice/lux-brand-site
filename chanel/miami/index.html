Project Overview

A static Chanel boutique build-out in Miami, priced at rough-costing fidelity.

Interior construction, finishes, fixtures, and millwork coordinated to Chanel global retail standards and Miami-Dade permitting. Figures in this document are a rough costing exercise, not a formal bid.

The project is a Chanel retail environment at [UPDATE BEFORE SENDING] SF in Miami, Florida. The work is a static boutique build-out — not a pop-up, not a touring installation — delivered inside an existing tenant envelope under Miami-Dade County permitting and Florida hurricane code. Scope covers interior construction from demolition through finishes, millwork fabrication and installation to Chanel-approved shop drawings, and mechanical, electrical, and plumbing modifications required to support the new layout. FF&E, AV, landlord base-building work, and permit fees sit outside this number and are called out explicitly in Section 02.

Numbers firm up when drawings are at 100% CDs, when the Miami-Dade permit set is reviewed, and when long-lead millwork and storefront subs return priced scope. 03 —
Approach

Single-shift schedule, dedicated site team, preconstruction lock before mobilization.

Four operating principles govern the Miami build-out. Each is priced into the general conditions line or called out as a separate assumption.

01

Single-Shift Schedule.

[UPDATE BEFORE SENDING] Standard hours assumed against the landlord rules for the Miami tenant space. General conditions, labor, and sub pricing in this exercise reflect a weekday first-shift sequence with limited Saturday work for millwork install and finish touch-up. Off-hours, night, or weekend extended work is priced separately if the landlord requires it or if the schedule compresses after permit review.

The consequence: a single-shift number is the lowest defensible figure. Any requirement for mall-hour restrictions, night freight, or accelerated closeout will be documented as a separate schedule assumption with its own cost premium carried transparently in the formal bid.

02

Dedicated Site Team.

[UPDATE BEFORE SENDING] A Project Manager and Site Superintendent are assigned full-time to the Miami boutique for the duration of construction. Neither is split across concurrent projects. The PM owns submittals, RFIs, procurement, and budget; the Superintendent owns trade sequence, quality control, and daily coordination with the Chanel Client Representative.

Full-time staffing is priced into the general conditions line. This is how Chanel brand standards are protected during millwork install, stone setting, and finish mock-up review. The Superintendent runs the punch list against Chanel static-store criteria before brand walkthrough.

03

Preconstruction Alignment.

[UPDATE BEFORE SENDING] Early engagement with the Chanel design team, the architect of record, and the Client Representative locks scope, finish selections, and long-lead procurement before mobilization. Long-lead items — storefront glazing to Miami-Dade impact spec, stone slabs, lacquered millwork, and specialty lighting — are identified and released against deposit before the construction start.

Preconstruction closes with a scope-and-assumptions memo signed by Chanel. Nothing moves to buyout until that memo is resolved. This is where the rough costing exercise converts into a formal bid with real numbers against a real drawing set.

04

Subcontractor Vetting.

[UPDATE BEFORE SENDING] Miami subcontractors are selected from a qualified pool with prior luxury-retail experience at Chanel-tier finish tolerance. Millwork installers, stone setters, specialty plasterers, and lighting electricians are prequalified against past boutique work for comparable brands in the South Florida market. Subs without documented luxury-retail history are not invited to bid.

Vetting is done during preconstruction, not during buyout. The formal bid carries named subs against each trade line, not open allowances. This is the mechanism by which a rough costing figure becomes a defensible, brand-appropriate number at the buyout stage.
