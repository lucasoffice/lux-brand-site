01
Overview

49,200 SF across three floors in Beverly Hills, rotated in six phases.

A multi-floor office and workshop renovation for Chanel, phased half-floor at a time to preserve jeweler, watchmaker, polishing, and packaging operations throughout. Last substantial renovation dated to 2007.

The work covers approximately 49,200 square feet distributed across three floors of a single Beverly Hills facility owned and operated by Chanel. Scope resolved to finishes-level construction. Demolition of existing interior finishes was confirmed. Wall reconfiguration opens the dead pockets between jewelers, watchmakers, the polishing team, and packaging. New flooring was carried throughout. Paint was carried in full. Acoustical tile patching closed the work out. Electrical relocation suited the revised layout. No structural work was carried. No exterior work was carried. No facade or signage work was carried.

Execution was structured as six half-floor phases, running approximately five to eight weeks each, with a one-week overlap between consecutive phases to compress the program. Demolition was scheduled off-hours in every phase to avoid disruption to the active jewelry, watch, polish, and packaging operations that run the building. Framing, finishes, and electrical proceeded on standard hours to hold labor cost predictable. First floor absorbed storage relocated from the second, which drove a heavier demolition figure on that level. The phasing logic was built around operational continuity, not around trade sequencing convenience.

02
Scope

Finishes-level work across two renovated floors, with Floor 1 absorbing relocated storage.

Demolition, wall reconfiguration, flooring, paint, ACT patching, and electrical relocation. Furniture, stainless workbenches, drawings, and permit fees excluded.

Included
  1. DemolitionDemolition confirmed across all renovated floors of interior office finishes. First floor largely demoed to absorb storage relocated from Floor 2. All demolition scheduled off-hours to protect confidentiality and operational continuity.
  2. Wall constructionNew and relocated partitions opened flow between departments and created new office pockets. Linear footage held at 250 LF per floor as a budgetary placeholder pending plan confirmation and final schematic release.
  3. FlooringCarpet tile at 20-inch by 20-inch specified across office areas, white rubber VCT in watchmaker zones, and epoxy or painted concrete across roughly 70 percent of Floor 1 where warehouse and storage functions landed after relocation.
  4. PaintFull coat of paint carried throughout all renovated areas. No wallpaper, no specialty coatings, no feature walls in the base scope. Color palette aligned to existing Chanel interior standard.
  5. ACT ceilingPatch, repair, and selective replacement of acoustical ceiling tile around new wall work. Existing grid salvaged in place wherever condition permitted. Full grid replacement excluded from base.
  6. Electrical relocationManipulation of existing electrical to suit new layouts, carried as a lump sum per floor pending final drawing release. No new service upgrade carried. Panel work excluded from base.
  7. Phased executionSix phases, half-floor at a time, with approximately one week of overlap between consecutive phases. Operational continuity held across jeweler, watchmaker, polish, and packaging functions throughout the program.
  8. DumpstersApproximately five dumpsters per phase budgeted for demolition and finishes swap-outs. Staging confirmed against the off-hours access window and the loading dock schedule.
Excluded
  1. FurnitureWorkstation manipulation and furniture procurement carried under a separate vendor once design was finalized. Not included in the base GC scope. LBCO coordinated install windows only.
  2. WorkbenchesStainless steel workbenches and jeweler stations handled under Chanel's specialty equipment budget. LBCO coordinated installation windows against the phasing plan but did not carry the equipment cost.
  3. DrawingsArchitectural and design drawings remained client scope. LBCO provided build-spec input but did not produce stamped drawings of any kind under this engagement.
  4. PermitsBase approach assumed finishes-level work with no permit required. If permitting triggered review, drawings scaled up by approximately $15,000 to $20,000 and were added as a documented change.
  5. SprinklersExcluded from base scope unless layout shifts triggered sprinkler head relocation. Final trigger confirmed against the approved floor plan during drawing review.
03
Approach

Ballpark first, drawings later, off-hours where it matters.

Seven coordinated moves hold the budget defensible, the site confidential, and the operation running through six phases.

01

Ballpark-first discovery

A defensible budget range was delivered before full architectural drawings were commissioned. Leadership reviewed the figure against the $1M to $5M internal spread and decided whether to proceed on that basis. Drawing investment followed the decision, not the other way round. The exercise was scoped to answer the go or no-go question first.

02

Off-hours walkthrough

Site walkthrough and Matterport scan were scheduled after business hours to preserve confidentiality from on-site jewelers, watchmakers, polishers, and packaging staff. Access was coordinated through Jacquelyn Albano only. No on-site staff were briefed in advance of leadership's internal communication plan.

03

Six-phase rotation

Work ran half a floor at a time across six phases. Each phase ran Demo, then Build and Sand across two weeks, then Finishes across two weeks, then Punch across one week. A one-week overlap carried between consecutive phases to compress the total program without collapsing trade sequencing.

04

Off-hours demo

Demolition ran off-hours in every phase to avoid disruption to the active workshop. Framing, finishes, and electrical proceeded on standard hours to control labor cost. Phase 1 was documented as one week of off-hours demo against a standard-hours build-out sequence. The split held across all six phases.

05

Finishes-level permit posture

Work was positioned as finishes-only to avoid full permitting. The posture was revisited if the electrical threshold or sprinkler relocation triggered municipal review. The permit question was routed through Luke Shank and the electrician before final pricing closed. An additional drawing line item was held in reserve.

06

Separate furniture track

Furniture and specialty workstation procurement were carved into a parallel vendor track so GC scope stayed clean. Stainless workbenches and jeweler stations sat inside Chanel's equipment budget. LBCO coordinated install windows only. The separation protected the unit-rate structure of the core estimate.

07

Living budget tool

A shared micro-site carried the running budget between revisions. Stakeholder input from Chris, Brian, and Jacquelyn was collected in one place, reducing the telephone-game pivots that typically track a pre-drawing estimate. Every number carried its underlying rate alongside it so revisions resolved at the input level.

04
Observations

Eight site conditions shaping the estimate.

Documented from the April 2026 planning call in Springfield, NJ. Each condition maps to a line item or an exclusion.

01

Dead Space and Broken Workflow

The existing floor plan carries numerous small office pockets that produced a disjointed flow between jewelers, watchmakers, the polishing team, and operations packaging. Wall reconfiguration addressed this directly across the renovated floors at 250 LF per floor.

02

Freight Elevator Limitation

Freight served only the first floor. Storage held on the second floor required manual uncrating, stair transport, and re-crating at ship time. Storage relocated to Floor 1 to close the loop and eliminate the manual handoff.

03

Conflicting Internal Budget

Internal estimates ranged from $1M to $1.5M on the operations side up to $5M at the executive level. Jacquelyn Albano required an accurate ballpark to align leadership before drawings were commissioned.

04

Shifting Second-Floor Schematic

Three different schematics for the second floor remained in circulation. Operations later clarified that two floors, not three, carried the heavy renovation, with two-thirds of Floor 2 allocated to watchmakers and the remainder to new offices.

05

Confidentiality Required

The project was not to be raised with on-site staff under any circumstances. Walkthrough, Matterport scan, and all discovery traffic ran off-hours. Communication routed through Jacquelyn Albano exclusively until leadership was ready to communicate.

06

White Rubber VCT in Watchmaker Zones

Watchmaker areas required a specific white rubber VCT product so dropped parts remained visible against the floor and bounced rather than broke on impact. Product specification provided by client. Install carried in base flooring scope.

07

2007 Renovation Baseline

Plans reviewed during the planning call indicated the space had not been substantially renovated in nearly two decades. Finish conditions, ACT grid, and electrical routing all reflected that vintage and informed the patching-versus-replacement split.

08

Non-Union Labor Market

The facility location is non-union, which held labor pricing more predictable than nearby NYC union zones. This carried directly into the $56 per square foot labor rate applied across all three floor subtotals without regional premium.
