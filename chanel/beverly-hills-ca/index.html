01
Overview

49,200 SF across three floors in Springfield, New Jersey.

Preliminary budgetary estimate for a multi-floor office and watchmaker workshop renovation. Scope narrowed in the April 15 follow-up to Floors 1 and 2; Floor 3 pricing preserved for reference only.

Scope covers 49,200 SF across three floors in Springfield, New Jersey. The work is a finishes-level renovation — interior demolition, wall reconfiguration, electrical relocation, new flooring in three finish systems, full repaint, and acoustical ceiling tile patching. Construction is sequenced across six half-floor phases with a one-week overlap between phases so trades roll floor-to-floor rather than demobilizing. Demolition runs off-hours throughout. Framing, finishes, and electrical run on standard hours to hold labor at open-shop rates, which the Springfield market supports and which the budget assumes.

The exercise exists to close a gap. The President quoted $5M a year ago. Operations quoted $1M to $2M in the same quarter. Leadership has not committed to a direction because the range is too wide to defend. This document delivers a credible, defensible number built from schematic sketches, a 250 LF per floor wall assumption, and vendor-confirmed unit rates — $4,186,770 carried across three floors, with Floors 1 and 2 confirmed active and Floor 3 preserved for reference per the April 15 call with Chris, the RVP of Operations. Overhead is held at 12%. Contingency is held at 5%. Both figures sit inside the grand total and are not adjustable after the fact.

Jacquelyn Albano is the single point of contact on the Chanel side. She coordinates internally between the VP of Operations, the master watchmaker, and the RVP of Ops. LBCO delivers through Luke Shank as principal and Sean as project lead, with Claudia referenced from the prior Miami engagement as the collaboration benchmark rather than staffed on Springfield. No marketing overture sits inside this number. No mission framing inflates it. The figure below is what a finishes-level renovation of the stated envelope costs under the assumptions listed, and the guardrails at the end of this document state exactly where the figure will move once drawings arrive, once linear footage is confirmed, and once the permit and sprinkler thresholds are resolved.

02
Scope

Included and excluded, itemized against the 49,200 SF envelope.

Finishes-level renovation — demolition, wall reconfiguration, electrical relocation, flooring, paint, ACT patching. Furniture, stainless steel workbenches, architectural drawings, and permit fees sit outside the base number.

Included — Nine Line Items

Interior demolition — scrape existing flooring, remove designated walls, and prepare ACT grid. Floor 1 runs largely full demo of vacated areas to convert to storage, performed off-hours to protect operational continuity.

Wall reconfiguration — new partition walls per finalized layout, budgeted at approximately 250 linear feet per floor pending plan confirmation. Floor 1 expected to run roughly half that linear footage given its storage conversion.

Carpet tile flooring — 20-inch square tiles at $25 per tile, equivalent to $9.00 per SF installed, in office areas across both active floors. A 15% adhesive uplift is carried as a separate line for budget clarity.

Specialty VCT flooring — white rubberized VCT in watchmaker and jeweler zones on Floor 2. Spec to be provided by Operations; unit rate held against comparables until the final product data sheet arrives.

Epoxy paint flooring — approximately 70% of Floor 1 receives ground and prepped concrete with an epoxy-style finish sized for warehouse and storage use. The remaining 30% holds existing finish or carpet tile depending on function.

Full repaint — paint throughout all renovated areas on both active floors, carried inside the $56/SF labor allowance. Paint only. Wallpaper explicitly excluded per Operations.

ACT patching — salvage and patch the existing acoustical ceiling tile grid where possible rather than full replacement. Replacement tile and grid pieces are carried at a salvage-plus-10% assumption.

Electrical relocation — lump sum $130,000 per floor for receptacle and circuit manipulation tied to the new wall layout. No major service upgrade assumed; panel work and service feeders are held flat.

Dumpsters and waste — approximately 5 dumpsters per phase for demolition debris and finish-stage waste swap-outs. Rolling schedule coordinated with the off-hours demo crew.

Excluded — Six Line Items

Furniture and workstation placement — deferred to a separate vendor. Client confirmed this scope sits outside the GC line until final layout is locked and the furniture vendor is under contract.

Stainless steel workbenches and jeweler stations — specialty equipment priced under a separate equipment budget by a dedicated vendor already identified by Operations.

Architectural and design drawings — client-side scope. LBCO estimates $15,000 to $20,000 if a permit submission ultimately requires a full drawing set stamped by an architect of record.

Permit fees and full permit drawings — base scope assumed to be a finishes-level project not requiring permit. Sprinkler and electrical thresholds to be confirmed; once triggered, both drawings and municipal fees are added.

Floor 3 — per the April 15 call with Chris, only Floors 1 and 2 are in active scope. Floor 3 pricing carried in the PDF rollup is preserved strictly for leadership reference.

Wallpaper — Operations confirmed paint only. No wall covering carried under any division; any future wallpaper scope prices as a change order against the finalized layout.

04
Workflow Context

Dead space and a broken floor-to-floor flow, not aesthetics, drove the exercise.

Current layout splits jewelers, watchmakers, polishing, and operations across poorly connected zones. The freight elevator serves Floor 1 only, forcing manual staging up and down stairs.

The floor plan is the problem. Jewelers sit separated from polishing. Watchmakers sit separated from operations and packaging. Dead zones fill the gaps between active areas, and the active zones bottleneck around them. The freight elevator terminates at Floor 1, which means every pallet, every bench reconfiguration, and every parts run between Floors 2 and 3 moves by hand up the stairwell. The renovation exists to fix the flow, not the finish. Every line item in the pricing section traces back to that single problem statement.

Three schematic options are in play for Floor 2. The master watchmaker drew them by hand at schematic level, each resolving the watchmaker-to-polishing adjacency in a different way. Team alignment between Jacquelyn, the VP of Operations, and the RVP of Ops is pending. None of the three have been priced individually; the base number assumes a 250 LF per floor wall budget that absorbs the variance between the three. Once the finalized schematic arrives, the wall line collapses against the actual linear footage and the budget firms in both directions.

White rubberized VCT was specified for a reason, not an aesthetic. Watchmakers drop parts. On carpet, small parts disappear into the pile and the watchmaker loses the component and the time to locate it. On hard ceramic tile, small parts shatter on impact and the component is destroyed outright. Rubberized VCT in white gives the watchmaker a visible field to find the dropped part and enough elastic return to recover it intact. The spec sheet is pending from Operations; the unit rate carried in pricing is held against industry comparables and will be confirmed the moment the product data sheet lands.

Open-shop labor is confirmed for the Springfield market. The $56/SF labor allowance for flooring install, paint, and ACT patching reflects that reality, as does the $130,000 per floor electrical lump. A union assumption on the same scope would add materially to the total across both labor and electrical, and the figure below would not be defensible in front of leadership asked to reconcile the $5M and $1M-to-$2M poles. The open-shop confirmation is what makes $4,186,770 the right number instead of the wrong one, and it is why the guardrail at the end of this document does not flag labor rate as a movable variable.

Phase 1 absorbs eight weeks to establish cadence with the demolition crew, the framer, and the electrician on a new site. Every subsequent phase compresses to five or six weeks once the trades are mobilized and rolling floor-to-floor. The approximately one-week overlap between phases is where the finish trades on the closing phase meet the demo crew on the opening phase, which is why demo is explicitly scheduled off-hours throughout and why the finish punch crew carries a separate week rather than sharing floor with demolition.

If Floor 3 is confirmed out of scope, Phases 5 and 6 drop and the program compresses to 21 working weeks with four overlap weeks instead of five. The construction subtotal drops correspondingly. All duration figures assume open-shop labor availability and the $130,000 per floor electrical lump holding against the final wall count.
