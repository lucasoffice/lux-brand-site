02
Read of the Project

Three conditions shape the Miami buildout.

Brand-standard finish tolerance, static site conditions, and Miami-specific permitting and assembly requirements.

01

Brand-standard finish tolerance.

Rolex boutiques require precision millwork, specialty lighting, and a brand-approved material palette sourced through vetted vendors. The finish tolerance is tighter than a conventional luxury retail fit-out, and the vendor list is closed — substitution requires Rolex approval in writing.

02

Static site, continuous sequence.

The project is delivered without phasing or after-hours constraints. Standard working hours apply across demo, rough-in, finishes, and punch. No shift differential and no weekend premium carried in the general conditions.

03

Miami market conditions.

Miami-Dade permitting, hurricane-rated assemblies, and the regional subcontractor pool shape both cost and schedule. Long-lead items — glazing, specialty ceiling systems, Rolex-supplied millwork — are tracked against the local delivery window rather than the original-of-record calendar.

04
Execution Model

Static site, single phase, millwork-led sequence.

Four decisions drive the schedule and the number.

01

Standard hours execution.

Static site mode allows standard daytime working hours across all trades. Demo, rough-in, finish, and punch run on a single shift without weekend or overnight work.

The general conditions reflect a straight-time cost basis. No shift differential is built into either labor or supervision.

Consequence — no shift differential carried in GCs.
02

Single-phase delivery, continuous sequence.

No phasing is required. Trades sequence continuously from demo through punch, with no remobilization, no temporary partitions, and no occupied-space protection carried.

Full-site access holds for the entire duration. The schedule lives or dies on predecessor completions, not on handover windows.

Consequence — no phasing premium, no temp-partition carry.
03

Early millwork coordination.

Shop drawings, field dimensions, and delivery logistics lock during preconstruction. The Rolex-approved millwork vendor is engaged before mobilization so casework arrives in sequence with the finished substrate.

Receiving, on-site storage, and install crews stage against a fixed millwork delivery calendar issued before demo starts.

Consequence — millwork installation drives the finish calendar.
04

Miami local trade partners.

Miami-based subcontractors with luxury retail experience are engaged early. Preconstruction pricing pulls from three-sub minimums across demo, finishes, and MEP, with buyout targeting the same pool.

Hurricane-rated assembly subs are selected from the regional pre-qualified list rather than imported.

Consequence — buyout risk localized, lead times honored.

09
Controls

Two controls protect the Rolex standard.

Documented QC against the Rolex brand book, and weekly transparent cost and schedule reporting.

01

Brand-Standard Quality Control.

Dedicated QC reviews run at three fixed gates — rough-in, finish, and punch. Each gate produces a signed inspection record measured against the Rolex brand book and the vetted-vendor specification sheets.

Deviations are logged with remediation scope and timeline before the next trade releases to the affected area. The site superintendent holds the sign-off, with PE concurrence required to close finish and punch gates.

QC records transfer to the closeout binder at turnover alongside the warranty package.

Owner — Lux PM
02

Weekly Cost and Schedule Reporting.

A weekly report issues every Friday covering RFIs open and closed, submittals pending and approved, change orders logged, and a rolling two-week look-ahead schedule against the baseline.

Cost variance is reported line-by-line against the subcontracted amount, with commentary on any item trending above the buyout number. Nothing moves in the background.

The report reaches the Rolex client contact by end-of-day each week for the duration of the project.
