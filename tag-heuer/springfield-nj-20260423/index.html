Overview

Two floors, six phases, 49,200 square feet at TAG Heuer Springfield.

Scope reduced from three floors to Floors 1 and 2 per RVP of Operations Chris. Floor 1 runs demo and storage conversion. Floor 2 holds the watchmakers, jewelers, polishing, and new offices.

Springfield is a multi-floor office and workshop renovation at TAG Heuer's northeastern operations facility. The project reconfigures flow between jewelers, watchmakers, polishing, and the operations and packaging team. Fragmented office pockets collapse into a single workshop plate. Dead space compresses. The color box — walls, floors, paint, ceilings, electrical — is the work. Furniture, stainless workbenches, and jeweler stations run on a parallel vendor track and never cross into the GC budget.

The building was last touched in 2007. Offices broke into small pockets over the intervening years as teams shifted and partitions went up ad-hoc. The freight elevator serves Floor 1 only, forcing Floor 2 storage to be unpacked, carried down stairs, re-crated, and routed out through the ground-floor dock. Three competing hand-drawn Floor 2 schematics sit on Jacquelyn's desk — blue lines mark proposed walls, all three originate with the master watchmaker, none are aligned. Watchmaker zones require a white rubberized VCT spec so dropped parts stay visible and bounce rather than shatter. That spec is a TAG deliverable and sits on the TAG Operations Team ledger.

This exercise replaces a $1.5M to $5M internal spread — Operations sitting at the floor, the President sitting at the ceiling — with a defensible figure built from unit rates and verified takeoffs. $4,186,770 all-in, overhead at 12 percent, contingency at 5 percent. The rollup carries three floors forward from the original estimate and will compress once Floor 3 is formally removed from scope. This document is not a formal bid. It is a costing exercise sized to hold a leadership conversation at TAG Heuer and move the project from guess to number.

The phasing logic is protective. Six half-floor rotations with a one-week overlap at every handoff keep trades productive and the watchmakers at their benches on the untouched half. Demolition runs off-hours per the confidentiality ask. Framing, finishes, and punch run on standard hours to hold labor at baseline. The permit posture is finishes-refresh. Sprinkler modifications and electrical load thresholds are the two trigger points and both sit under trade-partner review before any work starts at Springfield.

03
Approach

Six-phase half-floor rotation, demo off-hours, finishes-refresh permit posture.

Phasing is set to protect Springfield operations and hold the project below the leadership disclosure line until TAG Heuer is ready to brief the building.

3.1

Six-phase half-floor rotation.

Phase 1 runs Floor 1 first half. Phase 2 runs Floor 1 second half. Phases 3 and 4 rotate through Floor 2 in halves. A one-week overlap at each handoff keeps trades productive and eliminates the dead week between demo crew release and framing crew arrival.

The rotation preserves watchmaker capacity on the untouched half at every phase line. Jewelers and polishing stay live.

3.2

Demo off-hours, balance on standard hours.

Demolition executes off-hours to protect operations and confidentiality. Framing, finishes, and punch run on standard hours to hold labor cost at the baseline rate. The off-hours premium sits only on the demolition week at each phase.

The split saves roughly twenty percent against a fully-off-hours schedule without exposing the project to Springfield staff.

3.3

Finishes-refresh permit posture.

Scope positioned as a finishes refresh to avoid full permit submission. Sprinkler modifications and electrical load thresholds are the two trigger points that would force a filing and stamped drawing set.

Both thresholds under review with the licensed electrical and sprinkler trades before any work starts. Verification sits on Luke.

3.4

GC scope on the color box.

Walls, floors, paint, ceilings, electrical. Furniture and stainless stations run a parallel vendor track managed directly by the TAG team. The separation protects budget clarity on both sides.

It also blocks the change-order creep that furniture scope typically pulls into GC numbers mid-project.

3.5

Off-hours walkthrough or Matterport.

LBCO walks the building off-hours or captures a Matterport scan so Jacquelyn, Chris, Brian, and the RVP can review spatial conditions remotely. The scan surfaces nothing to Springfield staff until leadership approves disclosure.

The same scan feeds the takeoff refinement on wall linear footage and ceiling tile counts.

3.6

Living scope document.

This micro-site stays open to Jacquelyn, Chris, and Brian for asynchronous input between working sessions. Revisions publish after each call. No telephone-game scope drift between calls.

Every comment lands in the document and routes to the version log. The document is the single source of truth.

04
Site Observations

Eight conditions logged from the Springfield walk and planning calls.

Findings drive the phasing logic, the permit posture, and the schematic alignment task still open on Floor 2.

4.1

Current layout lacks flow.

Offices fragment into small pockets across both floors. Flow between jewelers, watchmakers, polishers, and the operations and packaging team is broken. Significant dead space runs through the north side of Floor 2 and the southeast corner of Floor 1.

4.2

Vertical logistics are broken.

Freight elevator serves Floor 1 only. Floor 2 storage gets unpacked at the shelf, carried down stairs, re-crated on Floor 1, then routed out through the freight dock. Labor-intensive and the single largest inefficiency in the building.

4.3

Schematics are preliminary.

Three hand-marked Floor 2 schematics exist. Blue lines mark proposed walls. All three originate with the master watchmaker. Team alignment still open between Jacquelyn, Chris, and the watchmaker. No formal drawing set produced under this exercise.

4.4

Scope reduced to two floors.

Per RVP of Operations Chris, the renovation now covers Floors 1 and 2 only. Floor 3 formally pending removal. Floor 1 holds demo and storage conversion. Floor 2 holds watchmakers, jewelers, polishing, and the new offices.

4.5

Building last touched in 2007.

Nineteen-year gap since the last renovation. Finishes are tired. Partitions went up ad-hoc as teams shifted. Electrical runs are legacy and require threshold review before the finishes-refresh posture locks in.

4.6

Budget expectations run wide.

Internal spread ranges from $1.5M at the Operations floor to $5M at the President ceiling. This exercise replaces the guess range with a defensible $4,186,770 built from unit rates, verified takeoffs, overhead at twelve percent, and contingency at five percent.

4.7

Specialty flooring for watchmakers.

Watchmaker zones require white rubberized VCT that allows small dropped parts to stay visible on the floor and bounce rather than shatter. Spec is a TAG Operations Team deliverable. Install allowance carried at the carpet rate pending the spec drop.

4.8

Permit strategy — finishes refresh.

Project positioned as a finishes refresh to avoid full permit submission. Sprinkler modifications and electrical threshold crossings are the two trigger points. Both under trade review before any phase kicks off. Verification sits on Luke.
