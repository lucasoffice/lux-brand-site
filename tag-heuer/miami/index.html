01
OVERVIEW

Two floors, 32,800 square feet, watchmakers on Floor 2.

Scope refined from the original three-floor concept down to Floors 1 and 2. Floor 1 carries storage with partial epoxy and a 30% office pocket; Floor 2 houses the watchmakers on specialty white rubberized VCT.

TAG Heuer's Springfield, NJ facility last saw meaningful renovation circa 2007. Acoustical ceiling tile, MEP, and interior finishes are effectively original to that vintage, and the current floor plan carries dead pockets and small offices that interrupt the flow between the jewelers, watchmakers, polishing team, and operations/packaging. Jacquelyn Albano is consolidating input from the VP of Operations, the master watchmaker, and the RVP of Ops into a single working scope so leadership can align on a number before any architectural drawings are commissioned.

Internal budget expectations ranged from roughly $1.5M on the Operations side to $5M on the President's side. This exercise exists to narrow that $1.5M–$5M spread into a credible middle figure — rough-order-of-magnitude, built bottom-up from per-floor construction and materials. The concept was originally three floors and 49,200 SF; the client has since reduced the working scope to Floors 1 and 2 at approximately 32,800 SF, and the pricing ladder below reflects that reduction while retaining the three-floor rollup as a reference.

Floor 1 converts to primarily in-and-out storage: roughly 70% epoxy-painted concrete after scrape and prep, with a 30% office pocket carrying walls, carpet tile, and standard finishes. Floor 2 houses the watchmakers on a specialty white rubberized VCT so that dropped jewelry components stay visible on the floor and bounce rather than break. Labor is confirmed open-shop, which opens up LBCO's New Jersey subcontractor base in the Short Hills / Springfield corridor.

03
APPROACH

Budget first, drawings second, demo off-hours only.

Seven operating principles governing this exercise — publish a credible ROM before committing architectural spend, phase in half-floor increments, keep demo off-hours and confidential, and consolidate middle-of-the-night client thoughts into a living document.

01

Budget-first, drawings-second.

A credible ROM lands first so leadership can decide whether to commission drawings at all. Architectural spend follows a decision, not the other way around — it is not committed against a project that might not proceed.

02

Phased execution, half-floor increments.

Six phases were originally scoped across three floors; four phases are now contemplated across two. A one-week overlap between phases keeps crews productive and the watchmakers live on the untouched half.

03

Off-hours demo, standard-hours build.

Only the noisy and disruptive demo runs off-hours, under the confidentiality protocol. Framing, finishes, and MEP run on standard hours to control premium-time labor cost across the balance of each phase.

04

Off-hours walkthrough or Matterport scan.

The space is captured discreetly so that stakeholders can review remotely without raising awareness on the floor. Matterport is the backup capture method if an off-hours site visit cannot be coordinated.

05

Living working document.

A shared micro-site where Jacquelyn, Chris, and Brian post middle-of-the-night thoughts. Luke consolidates those entries into versioned revisions so the scope tightens without a constant meeting burden.

06

GC plus separate furniture vendor.

Responsibilities and risk stay clean: the GC delivers the envelope; a separate furniture and workstation vendor delivers the specialty equipment, jeweler stations, and final placement.

07

Proven NJ trade base.

LBCO carries established subcontractor relationships in the Short Hills / Springfield corridor. Open-shop labor plus local coverage drives both pricing competitiveness and schedule reliability on this job.

04
SITE CONSTRAINTS

Freight serves Floor 1 only, walkthrough must stay confidential.

Two operational realities shaping phasing and logistics: the freight elevator does not reach Floor 2, and leadership has not told on-site staff the renovation is being evaluated.

BLOCK A / FREIGHT BOTTLENECK

The freight elevator does not reach Floor 2.

Storage on Floor 2 today requires the full manual cycle — unpack, walk down the stairs, re-crate at ground level, re-load onto the freight at Floor 1. Every shipment in and out of the second floor pays that tax.

The friction is a core driver of the renovation. Moving storage primarily to Floor 1 lets the freight do the work it was built to do, and frees Floor 2 for the watchmakers who should not be sharing circulation with pallets.

Phasing logic follows directly: Floor 1 gets the heavier destruction pass and the epoxy-painted concrete, Floor 2 gets the specialty rubberized VCT and the office pocket. The two floors are sequenced so storage relocation does not block production.

BLOCK B / CONFIDENTIALITY

On-site staff have not been informed.

Leadership has not told Springfield staff that a renovation is being evaluated. The site walkthrough therefore runs off-hours, coordinated directly with Jacquelyn, with no photography of active production areas at any point in the capture.

A Matterport scan is the backup capture method if an off-hours window cannot be coordinated. The scan is captured discreetly and reviewed remotely by stakeholders without further on-site visits.

All documentation — this micro-site included — is audience-controlled. The LBCO team is not introduced on the floor until Jacquelyn authorizes it under the separate next-step action.

GUARDRAILS

This is a ROM — furniture, equipment, and drawings are carved out.

Two constraints bracket the number. Figures are ROM based on sketches and a planning call; specialty equipment and design drawings sit under separate budgets.

01 / BUDGETARY EXERCISE — NOT A FORMAL BID

Scope, LF, MEP, and VCT spec are unconfirmed.

All figures are rough-order-of-magnitude numbers built from sketches and a planning call. Scope, wall linear footage, MEP impact, and the specialty flooring spec must be confirmed against drawings and a walkthrough before any figure is treated as a commitment.

The ROM exists to help leadership decide whether to commission drawings, not to price a committed build. A formal bid follows plan confirmation, walkthrough capture, and the republish decision under Next Step 09.

02 / FURNITURE, EQUIPMENT, AND DRAWINGS CARVED OUT

Stainless benches, jeweler stations, and design sit outside.

Stainless steel workbenches, jeweler stations, furniture manipulation, and architectural/design drawings are explicitly excluded from this base number. They are carried under TAG Heuer's equipment budget and the client architect's fee, respectively — not under GC scope.

LBCO supports the architect with build-spec input and coordinates the furniture vendor in the field, but does not carry either line financially. That boundary is what keeps the GC number legible and the risk lines clean.
