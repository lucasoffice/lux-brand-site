02 Scope of Work

Finishes-only renovation of office and watchmaker floors.

Ten inclusions and five exclusions. Specialty white rubberized VCT in watchmaker areas, carpet tile in office areas, epoxy-painted concrete across ~70% of the first floor.

Included (10)
  • Interior Demolition — Full demo of interior finishes, performed off-hours to avoid operational disruption; estimated ~1 week per phase.
  • Wall Reconfiguration — Selective removal and new stud-and-drywall walls to open dead space and improve watchmaker / ops workflow.
  • Flooring / Carpet Tile — 20 in x 20 in carpet tile (~$9/SF installed) in office areas, including adhesive.
  • Flooring / Specialty VCT — White rubberized VCT in watchmaker work areas per client spec — dropped watch components must be visible against the surface and must bounce rather than break. Spec to be provided by watchmaker team.
  • Flooring / Epoxy Paint — ~70% of first floor to receive scraped and epoxy-painted concrete for warehouse and storage use.
  • Paint — Coat of paint throughout renovated areas (included in $/SF finishes labor); no wallpaper.
  • ACT Ceiling — Patch, replace, and reinstall acoustical ceiling tile and grid around new wall locations.
  • Electrical Relocation — Manipulation of existing electrical to suit new layout, budgeted per floor — not a full re-feed.
  • Dumpsters & Debris Removal — Approximately five dumpsters per phase to support demo and construction turnover.
  • Phasing Logistics — Six-phase sequence at half-floor increments with ~1 week overlap between phases; potential flex to full-floor phases pending final two-floor scope.
Excluded (5)
  • Furniture Manipulation — Moving and reinstalling existing furniture deferred to a separate vendor scope until design is finalized.
  • Stainless Steel Workstations & Jeweler Benches — Specialty equipment procurement handled under a separate equipment budget.
  • Architectural / Design Drawings — Drawings to be produced by client's architect. Lux will support with build specs but will not carry design cost.
  • Permit-Triggered Scope — Base estimate assumes finishes-only work without permit. Sprinkler modifications or electrical work above threshold would trigger permit drawings at approximately $15K–$20K add.
  • Sitework / Facade / Exterior — No exterior or base-building work included.
03 Execution Approach

Off-hours demo, standard-hours build, open-shop NJ labor.

Six-phase sequence with one-week overlaps compresses total duration to ~33 weeks. Site walkthroughs scheduled off-hours per client discretion request.

01

Finishes-only filing posture.

Classify work as finishes to avoid permit submission where possible. Sprinkler and electrical load thresholds confirmed with subs before scope locks to protect the no-permit assumption.

02

Off-hours demo, standard-hours build.

Demo runs off-hours to keep jeweler, watchmaker, polishing, and packaging operations undisturbed. Framing, finishes, and electrical run standard hours to control labor cost against open-shop rates.

03

Half-floor phasing with overlap.

Six phases sequenced floor-by-floor with a one-week overlap at each handoff. Total duration compresses to approximately 33 weeks from Phase 1 start to Phase 6 punch.

04

Open-shop New Jersey labor.

Confirmed open-shop (non-union) market. Lux leverages existing Short Hills and North Central NJ subcontractor relationships — the same base used on comparable office renovations in the corridor.

05

Live micro-site for stakeholder input.

Lux maintains a shared hub so Chris, Brian, and other TAG Heuer stakeholders can post scope input asynchronously. Avoids game-of-telephone revisions and keeps a single source of truth for the evolving program.

06

Matterport or off-hours walkthrough.

Full 3D scan of the space captured once so stakeholders can mark up dead space and flow issues remotely. Reduces the number of in-person visits required before architect engagement.

04 Phasing Logistics

Six half-floor phases with one-week handoff overlap.

Phasing keeps watchmaker, polishing, and packaging workflows operational while the freight elevator (first floor only) dictates material staging.

Half-floor phasing exists because the freight elevator reaches only the first floor, and because jewelers, watchmakers, polishing, and packaging teams currently move inventory between floors throughout the working day. Operations cannot pause for the duration of a floor, and staging material on a full-floor basis would block the workflow corridors the ops team relies on. Splitting each floor in half preserves a working half while the adjacent half is demo'd, framed, and finished.

The overlap mechanic is a single week at each phase boundary after Phase 1. Demo on the incoming half runs off-hours for the week the outgoing half is in punch; framing and finishes on the incoming half then move to standard hours once punch on the outgoing half clears. Phase 1 is front-loaded at 8 weeks to absorb mobilization; Phases 2–4 run 6 weeks each; Phases 5–6 run 5 weeks each. Total duration compresses to approximately 33 weeks from Phase 1 start to Phase 6 punch.

If Chris confirms the two-floor scope, the sequence collapses from six half-floor phases to four and trims approximately 10–12 weeks from the total.
