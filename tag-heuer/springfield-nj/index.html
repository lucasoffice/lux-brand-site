01
Understanding

What we heard on the planning call, what we walked, and what shaped the estimate.

01

Three floors, ~16,400 SF each

Client initially described three floors at roughly 16,400 SF per floor (~49,200 SF total). Subsequent call narrowed active renovation to floors 1 and 2, with floor 1 largely converted to storage and warehouse use, and floor 2 rebuilt for watchmakers with new offices in the mix.

02

Dead space and poor workflow today

Current layout creates broken flow between jewelers, watchmakers, polishing, and ops packaging. The freight elevator only serves floor 1, forcing manual crate handling up and down stairs from floor 2 storage.

03

Specialty flooring for watchmakers

Watchmaker zones require specific white rubberized VCT so dropped components are visible and bounce rather than break. Remaining office areas use carpet tile; roughly 70% of floor 1 can be epoxy-painted concrete for warehouse and pallet use.

04

Phased, mostly daytime work

Per the updated call, overnight work is not required. Demo and build proceed during business hours with phasing. The plan contemplates six phases in half-floor increments with ~1-week overlaps, totaling ~33 weeks.

05

Open shop market

Springfield is confirmed non-union. Lux carries strong Short Hills and North-Central NJ subcontractor relationships, which shortens mobilization and keeps pricing competitive through the phases.

06

Budget sensitivity and internal alignment

Internal estimates have ranged from $1.5M to $5M depending on who is asked. The goal here is a credible ballpark the client can use to align leadership before committing to architectural drawings.

07

Drawings not yet produced

Only schematic sketches from the master watchmaker exist today, with three competing second-floor options. Lux is building the estimate against a documented set of assumptions until plans are finalized.

08

Furniture and equipment carved out

Stainless steel workbenches, jeweler stations, and furniture manipulation are excluded from base scope and will be handled by a separate vendor. This keeps the GC number clean as the layout evolves.

03
Revisions

What changed between the first pass and version five.

Scope reduced from 3 floors to 2

Client confirmed only floors 1 and 2 are in scope. Floor 3 removed from active renovation estimate.

Overnight work requirement dropped

Daytime execution confirmed acceptable, reducing labor premiums previously carried for night work.

Floor 1 flooring strategy revised

Approximately 70% of floor 1 switched from carpet tile to epoxy-painted concrete for warehouse and pallet use. Remaining 30% office areas retain carpet and walled offices.

Specialty VCT called out for watchmaker zones

Added budget line for white rubberized VCT in watchmaker areas on floor 2, replacing the generic carpet assumption in those zones.

Carpet tile pricing corrected

Updated from the initial $25/tile assumption to ~$9.00/SF installed to reflect realistic 20×20 carpet tile pricing.

Furniture manipulation pulled from base

Removed the $60/SF furniture manipulation line from base scope. Will be re-scoped once layout is finalized and handled by a separate vendor.

Permit posture clarified

Positioned as finishes-only scope to avoid a full permit. Sprinkler and electrical thresholds flagged as the two triggers that could force a permit, with a ~$15–20K drawing contingency if required.

05
Guardrails

Two commitments holding the scope and the number steady through design development.

01

Phased execution that protects live operations

Six half-floor phases with ~1-week overlaps totaling ~33 weeks, about eight months including a two-week holiday buffer. Demo is scheduled to minimize disruption to watchmakers, jewelers, polishing, and ops packaging teams running through the renovation.

02

Budget discipline with a clean carve-out

Base GC scope excludes furniture, stainless steel workbenches, and jeweler stations so the construction number stays defensible as design evolves. Carpet tile pricing was corrected mid-process from $25/tile to ~$9.00/SF installed, scrubbing the assumption before it reached leadership.
