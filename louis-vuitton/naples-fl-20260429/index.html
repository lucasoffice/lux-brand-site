01 — Overview
Project Dossier

A defensible number to replace the $1M-versus-$5M gap.

Two conflicting executive estimates triggered this exercise. Lux Brand Co was asked to deliver a budgetary framework Jacquelyn Albano can carry into the next leadership conversation.

BrandLouis Vuitton
LocationNaples, Florida
Square Footage49,200 SF across three floors
Year2026

The President quoted the Louis Vuitton Naples program at approximately $5M. Operations placed it nearer $1.5–2M. Jacquelyn Albano sits between those poles as the central filter — the single voice translating between operations, the in-house master watchmaker, and the Lux Brand Co team. Her brief was direct. Replace the gap with a number that holds.

This document is that number, presented as a rough costing exercise rather than a formal bid. No architectural drawings exist yet. The schematics in circulation were marked up by the master watchmaker, and three competing second-floor options remain in play. The framework below was built to be defended in a leadership room, refined against a drawing set, and not confused with a contract figure.

The figure resolves at $4,186,770 across three floors before any optional permitted-path additions. It assumes open-shop labor, the latest direction permitting day-shift build, and a finishes-only permit posture. Each of those three assumptions is named, sourced, and isolated below so any one can be adjusted without rebuilding the model.

03 — Approach
Method & Sequence

Six phases, half a floor at a time, day-shift build with off-hours demo.

Originally all off-hours; the latest client direction permits daytime build, which materially reduces labor premium. Walkthroughs remain off-hours to preserve the confidential posture.

01

Six-phase rollout, half a floor at a time.

Each phase covers half of a floor, sequenced bottom up. Phases overlap by roughly one week so the trade base stays continuously deployed and the program duration compresses.

02

Off-hours demo, day-shift build.

The original posture was fully off-hours. The latest direction permits daytime build, which lifts the labor premium and shortens the calendar. Demolition stays off-hours to keep the operation undisturbed.

03

Budget first, drawings second.

Lux Brand Co delivers a defensible per-SF and lump-sum framework now so leadership can decide whether to fund a drawing package against a real number rather than a guess.

04

Permit avoidance where defensible.

The work is positioned as finishes-only to avoid a full permit cycle. Sprinkler modifications and electrical scope above threshold are flagged as the two vulnerabilities that would force a permitted path. Confirmation with the electrician is queued under Next Steps before scope freeze.

05

Live walkthrough or Matterport capture.

An off-hours site capture gives every stakeholder one shared visual record. The scan removes ambiguity from scope conversations held remotely.

06

Stakeholder input through this micro-site.

Section eight collects input from Chris, Brian, and other internal stakeholders against a single document. The intent is to reduce telephone-game revisions and keep scope drift visible. Comments thread under each section so the audit trail remains intact through the next leadership review.

07

GC line kept clean of FF&E.

Furniture and stainless workstations were carved out so the GC scope stays a color-box base price that compares directly against the executive estimates without distortion.

04 — Constraints
Operational Reality

Freight elevator stops at one; workflow disconnects drove the reorganization.

The renovation is not aesthetic. Jeweler, watchmaker, polishing, and ops packaging flows are disconnected, and second-floor storage currently must be hand-carried down stairs and re-crated to reach the freight route.

The freight elevator reaches Floor One only. Inventory and equipment held on Floor Two must be hand-carried down the stairwell, re-crated at grade, and routed through the freight bay — a cost the operation absorbs every working day. The freight constraint is the single largest reason the reorganization exists, and it sets the priority order for which workflows move where on the second floor.

Four workflows currently cross each other rather than run parallel. Jeweler. Watchmaker. Polishing. Operations packaging. The in-house team identified material dead space along the seams between those flows, particularly where polishing and packaging share a corridor that neither was designed around. The brief is to resequence the floor plate so the four functions adjoin in working order rather than in legacy order.

Three competing schematics for Floor Two are in circulation. Each was marked up by the in-house master watchmaker. None has been adopted. The alignment is internal-team-driven and sits ahead of any formal drawing release, which is why this exercise treats the second-floor wall LF as a variable rather than a fixed line.

The bench needs sightline to polishing and a clean run to packaging. The current floor does neither. In-house Master Watchmaker

09 — Guardrails
Cost-Sensitive Thresholds

Wall linear footage and the permit path are the two cost-sensitive thresholds.

Each additional 100 LF of new wall adds approximately $18,500 per floor. Sprinkler modifications or electrical scope above threshold force a permitted path and roughly $15–20K of additional drawing cost.

01

Wall LF is the largest remaining cost variable.

The base model assumes 250 LF of new wall per floor at $185 per LF. Each additional 100 LF adds approximately $18,500 per floor and roughly $55,500 across the three-floor rollup. A move to full private offices in lieu of cubicles will exceed the current allowance and should trigger a model rebuild rather than a contingency draw. Confirmation against the drawing set is the single fastest way to tighten the figure.

02

Permit path is finishes-only, with two named vulnerabilities.

The base model carries a finishes-only permit posture. Sprinkler modifications and electrical scope above threshold are the two vulnerabilities that would force a permitted path. A permitted path adds roughly $15–20K of drawing cost and extends the schedule by the local plan-review cycle. Electrician confirmation is queued under Next Step 09 and must close before scope freeze.
